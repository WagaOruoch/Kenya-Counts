# The Question

## Primary Question
**"Has Kenya's tax money — collected since devolution in 2013 — translated into meaningfully better and more equally distributed public services for ordinary citizens?"**

---

## Why This Question Matters

In 2013, Kenya made a historic constitutional commitment: devolve power and resources to 47 counties, fund public services more equitably, and make government accountable to citizens at the local level. To finance this ambition, the Kenya Revenue Authority (KRA) was tasked with collecting more tax revenue year on year.

Eleven years later, Kenyans are paying more tax than at any point in the country's history. Yet in 2024, young Kenyans took to the streets — and some lost their lives — protesting a Finance Bill that would increase their tax burden further. Their implicit question was not just about the new bill. It was about the last decade: *What did we get for what we already gave?*

This project attempts to answer that question with data.

---

## The Sub-Questions

### Chapter 1 — The National Ledger (Taxpayer's Return)
1. How much has Kenya collected in tax revenue each year from 2013 to 2024?
2. How has the national budget been allocated across healthcare, education, and infrastructure over the same period?
3. Has actual government expenditure matched budget allocations — or is money promised but unspent?
4. Have measurable public service outcomes improved at a rate proportional to revenue growth?
5. What is the "return" a citizen has received for each additional shilling collected in tax?

### Chapter 2 — The County Divide (County Inequality)
1. How much equitable share funding has each of the 47 counties received since 2013?
2. Do counties that receive more funding deliver better health, education, and poverty outcomes?
3. Which counties are overperforming relative to their allocation — and which are underperforming?
4. Has the gap between the best and worst performing counties narrowed or widened since devolution?
5. Where you are born in Kenya — does it still determine what the government delivers to you?

---

## What This Project Will and Will Not Claim

**It will:**
- Show correlations between revenue, spending, and outcomes
- Identify patterns of over and underperformance
- Present the data transparently with all sources cited
- Let the evidence speak without political agenda

**It will not:**
- Claim to prove corruption or misappropriation
- Make causal claims without sufficient evidence
- Represent any political party or movement
- Pretend data alone tells the complete story — context will always be provided

---

## The Hypothesis Going In

*Tax revenue has grown significantly since 2013, but the improvement in public service delivery has not kept pace — and the benefits of devolution have been distributed unevenly, with a citizen's county of residence remaining a strong predictor of the quality of services they receive.*

This hypothesis may be proven, disproven, or complicated by the data. All three outcomes are valuable.

---

## Success Criteria

This project will be considered complete and successful when:
- A reader with no data background can understand the findings
- A researcher can reproduce every finding from the raw data
- The story can be told in a single dashboard, a single report, and a single README
- At least one Kenyan media outlet, NGO, or policy body would find it credible and useful
